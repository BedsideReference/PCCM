Approach to Shock

Recognize → classify → resuscitate → tailor · adult · pocket reference
Recognize

Shock = Circulatory Failure act before hypotension

Inadequate tissue perfusion / O₂ delivery relative to demand → anaerobic metabolism & cellular dysfunction. Hypotension is a late sign — compensated shock can have a normal BP.
Three windows of perfusion
  • Skin — mottling, cap refill > 3 s, cool/clammy (warm in early distributive)
  • Kidney — urine output < 0.5 mL/kg/h
  • Brain — new agitation, confusion, obtundation
Supporting signs
  • Lactate > 2 mmol/L (marker of hypoperfusion + severity)
  • Tachycardia, narrow pulse pressure, ↑ shock index (HR/SBP > 0.9)
  • MAP < 65, SBP < 90, or ↓ > 40 from baseline

Every Intervention Has a Lever

MAP = CO × SVR  ·  CO = HR × SV  ·  SV = preload · contractility · afterload
Fluids → preload · inotropes → contractility · rate/rhythm control → HR · vasopressors → SVR · relieve obstruction → restores filling/output. Name the low variable, pull that lever.
Classify — the four mechanisms

Four Types mixed shock is common

Hypovolemic↓ preload — volume loss. Hemorrhage, GI losses, burns, DKA, third-spacing.
CardiogenicPump failure. MI, arrhythmia, decompensated HF, valve/mechanical, myocarditis.
Distributive↓ SVR — vasodilation. Sepsis (most common), anaphylaxis, neurogenic, adrenal.
ObstructiveMechanical block to filling/output. Tension PTX, tamponade, massive PE, auto-PEEP.
Patients often have > 1 mechanism (e.g., septic + cardiogenic, or hemorrhage + obstruction). Re-classify as data returns.

Cardiogenic Profiles — Perfusion × Congestion Stevenson 2×2

Warm & DryPerfused, no congestion — compensated. Optimize oral HF therapy; find the trigger.
Warm & WetPerfused but congested — diurese, vasodilate (nitroglycerin), NIV for pulmonary edema.
Cold & DryHypoperfused, not congested — cautious fluid challenge / inotrope; exclude hypovolemia / over-diuresis.
Cold & Wet ★Hypoperfused + congested — classic cardiogenic shock. Inotrope + diuresis ± vasopressor ± mechanical support.
Perfusion (cold = hypoperfused): narrow pulse pressure, cool extremities, ↓ UOP, altered mentation, ↑ lactate. Congestion (wet = ↑ filling pressures): orthopnea, JVD, rales, edema, lung B-lines.

Bedside — RUSH / POCUS

  • Pump — contractility (hyperdynamic = distributive/hypovolemic; poor = cardiogenic), pericardial effusion/tamponade, RV strain/dilation (PE)
  • Tank — IVC (small/collapsing = hypovolemic; plethoric = cardiogenic/obstructive), lung B-lines (edema) or absent lung-sliding (PTX), FAST for free fluid
  • Pipes — aorta (AAA / dissection), femoral/popliteal DVT (PE source)
Resuscitate

Initial Resuscitation

  • ABCs; 2 large-bore IVs (± IO / central), O₂, continuous monitor
  • Labs: lactate + VBG, CBC, chem, coags, type & screen, cultures if infection; ECG; bedside US
  • Target MAP ≥ 65 (individualize higher in chronic HTN); resuscitate to perfusion, not a number — lactate clearance, UOP, mentation, cap refill
  • Fluids (hypovolemic/distributive): balanced crystalloid boluses; reassess responsiveness after each — don't reflexively give 30 mL/kg to cardiogenic/obstructive
  • Cardiogenic / obstructive: fluids can worsen — support the pump / relieve the obstruction instead
  • Source control: stop the bleeding, drain/decompress, antibiotics within 1 h for septic shock
Fluid responsiveness (dynamic > static): passive leg raise → ↑ CO ≥ 10%; PPV/SVV > 12% (controlled vent, sinus rhythm, VT ≥ 8 mL/kg); IVC variation; a 250–500 mL challenge. ~Half of unstable patients are not fluid-responsive — stop fluids that don't help.

Initial Workup — Send & Why

  • Lactate + VBG/ABG — hypoperfusion severity & clearance; acid–base
  • CBC + type & screen/cross — anemia/bleeding; prep for transfusion
  • Chem / creatinine, LFTs — end-organ perfusion (kidney, liver)
  • Coags ± fibrinogen — coagulopathy / DIC
  • Troponin, BNP, ECG — ischemia / cardiogenic / arrhythmia
  • Cultures (blood ×2, urine, source) before antibiotics if feasible
  • ScvO₂ (central line) — global O₂ supply–demand balance
  • Cortisol if adrenal crisis suspected · β-hCG · POCUS/CT for source
Vasopressors & inotropes

Pressor Access — Practical Notes

  • Don't delay pressors for a central line — norepinephrine runs safely peripherally short-term through a large, proximal (antecubital or above), freely-flowing IV; place central access when able
  • Extravasation (vasopressor into tissue) → stop the infusion, aspirate, elevate; antidote phentolamine 5–10 mg injected around the site (or topical nitroglycerin / terbutaline)
  • Arterial line for beat-to-beat pressure once on pressors — cuffs under-read at low flow
  • Titrate to MAP ≥ 65; wean the last-added agent first as perfusion recovers
Type-specific management

Hypovolemic / Hemorrhagic

  • Stop the loss — control bleeding (pressure, tourniquet, endoscopy, IR, OR)
  • Hemorrhage → blood products, balanced 1:1:1 (RBC:FFP:plt) massive transfusion; limit crystalloid (dilutional coagulopathy)
  • TXA within 3 h of trauma; correct coagulopathy; warm the patient (avoid lethal triad)
  • Trauma pre-control: permissive hypotension (SBP ~80–90) unless TBI

Cardiogenic

  • Revascularize — STEMI/ischemia → emergent cath; treat arrhythmia
  • Support: norepi for MAP + inotrope (dobutamine/milrinone) for CO
  • Cautious fluids; diurese if congested; NIV for pulmonary edema
  • Refractory → mechanical support (IABP / Impella / VA-ECMO); cardiology + shock team

Distributive — Septic

  • Antibiotics < 1 h + cultures + source control
  • Balanced crystalloid, reassessing responsiveness; norepi first, add vasopressin
  • Refractory → hydrocortisone 200 mg/day; consider angiotensin II
  • Track lactate clearance and perfusion; avoid over-resuscitation

Distributive — Anaphylaxis / Neurogenic

  • Anaphylaxis → IM epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg (repeat q5–15 min), fluids, epi infusion if refractory; adjunct antihistamines/steroids/albuterol
  • Neurogenic (spinal cord) → fluids + vasopressor (norepi/phenylephrine); atropine for symptomatic bradycardia; maintain spinal perfusion

Obstructive

  • Tension pneumothorax → immediate needle/finger thoracostomy, then chest tube
  • Cardiac tamponade → pericardiocentesis (± echo guidance)
  • Massive PE → systemic thrombolysis / catheter or surgical embolectomy; ECMO if arrest
  • Auto-PEEP in the vented patient → disconnect circuit, let the chest empty, then resume slow

Adrenal / Endocrine

  • Adrenal crisis — refractory hypotension + hyponatremia / hyperkalemia / hypoglycemia; steroid-dependent, withdrawn, or stressed. Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV then 200 mg/day — don't wait for cortisol
  • Myxedema coma → IV levothyroxine + hydrocortisone first (cover concurrent adrenal insufficiency)
  • Thyroid storm → β-blocker, thionamide, iodine (after thionamide), hydrocortisone

Endpoints & Monitoring

  • MAP ≥ 65; lactate clearance (> 10% / 2 h, trend to normal)
  • UOP ≥ 0.5 mL/kg/h; improving mentation & cap refill; ScvO₂ ≥ 70%
  • Reassess fluid responsiveness before each bolus; de-escalate pressors/fluids once perfusion restored
  • Re-image / re-examine if not improving — look for missed or second mechanism
Refractory — reassess

Not Responding? Think Again high-yield

  • Occult hemorrhage — chest / abdomen / pelvis / retroperitoneum / GI; re-scan, recheck Hgb
  • Missed obstruction — tamponade, tension PTX, massive PE, auto-PEEP (disconnect the vent)
  • Adrenal crisis — empiric hydrocortisone 100 mg
  • Severe acidosis — blunts catecholamine response; treat the cause
  • Hypocalcemia (esp. after massive transfusion) — replace calcium
  • Drug tox — β-blocker / CCB overdose → glucagon, high-dose insulin (HIET), calcium; local-anesthetic → lipid emulsion
  • Wrong / mixed diagnosis — re-POCUS, reconsider the shock type
  • Under-treated anaphylaxis — IM epinephrine, not just antihistamine
Don't be fooled: a normal blood pressure does not exclude shock (compensated) · treat perfusion, not the number · mixed shock is the rule in the sick · over-resuscitation with fluid causes harm · reassess the mechanism as data returns.
