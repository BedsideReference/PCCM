▲What changed in 2025 — four major advances

Updates the 2002 & 2013 classifications. Still stratifies patients first by major histological & radiological pattern, and keeps the distinction between a pattern (e.g. "NSIP") and a multidisciplinary diagnosis (e.g. "idiopathic NSIP").

1.Beyond idiopathic. A single system now covers both secondary and primary/idiopathic causes of each pattern. Secondary causes are listed first — exclude an underlying cause before calling a pattern idiopathic. New causes can be appended over time.
2.New patterns & terms. BIP (bronchiolocentric interstitial pneumonia) added as a major pattern; "HP" reserved for the multidisciplinary diagnosis, not a pattern. AIP → idiopathic DAD. DIP → AMP (alveolar macrophage pneumonia).
3.Interstitial vs alveolar-filling. Disorders split into interstitial patterns (further divided fibrotic vs non-fibrotic) and alveolar filling patterns.
4.Diagnostic confidence. Explicit confidence levels are applied to imaging patterns, biopsy patterns, and the multidisciplinary diagnosis — acknowledging uncertainty and the need to reassess over time.
Terminology evolution (2002 → 2013 → 2025)

2002 was IPF-centric (IPF vs non-IPF IIP). 2013 formalized NSIP as a major pattern and added PPFE. 2025 abandons the idiopathic-only frame, adds BIP, renames AIP→idiopathic DAD and DIP→AMP, separates interstitial vs alveolar-filling disorders, and integrates progressive pulmonary fibrosis (PPF) as a treatment-relevant behavior. The prefix "i" denotes idiopathic (e.g. iNSIP, iBIP, iDAD).

◱Mental map — how to think about an interstitial pneumonia

One funnel: name the pattern, exclude a secondary cause, attach a confidence, describe the behavior, then pull a management lever — with MDD as the hub throughout.

→Classification approach

Interstitial pneumonia = umbrella term for ~200 rare diseases affecting the interstitium and/or small airways and alveoli. "ILD" is interchangeable. Not covered here: pneumoconioses, sarcoidosis, vascular disorders, LAM, Langerhans cell histiocytosis, alveolar hemorrhage, infection, IPAF (separate document), and management.

Workflow: Interstitial pneumonia/ILD suspected on chest CT → assign a morphological pattern (interstitial: UIP, NSIP, BIP, DAD, PPFE, LIP · alveolar filling: OP, RB-ILD, AMP, rare AFPs · or combined / unclassifiable) → multidisciplinary discussion integrating clinical, molecular & pathological findings → clinical diagnosis, excluding secondary causes before calling it primary/idiopathic.

No pathognomonic test for most interstitial pneumonias. Confidence is applied to the imaging pattern, the biopsy pattern, and how likely a pattern represents a specific multidisciplinary diagnosis.

Practical note: clinicians often accept a ~70% confidence in IPF as sufficient to manage without biopsy. A BIP pattern with specific features (e.g. profuse centrilobular ground-glass nodules on CT, peribronchiolar granulomatous inflammation on biopsy) can convey high confidence for HP even without a clear exposure — worth stating explicitly in reports.

◎HRCT quick-reference

Interstitial patterns

  • Craniocaudal: lower — UIP, NSIP, LIP · diffuse — BIP, DAD · upper — PPFE
  • Axial: peripheral — UIP, NSIP, PPFE · subpleural sparing — NSIP · peribronchovascular — BIP, LIP
  • Honeycombing: UIP
  • Air trapping / three-density sign: BIP
  • Consolidation / apical pleural: PPFE
  • Cysts: LIP
  • Ground-glass + septal thickening: DAD

Alveolar filling patterns

  • Distribution: multifocal — OP, EP · focal — lipoid · diffuse — AMP, PAP
  • Density: consolidation — OP, EP · fat — lipoid · ground-glass — AMP, PAP
  • Emphysema: AMP
  • Septal thickening (crazy-paving): PAP
  • Aspiration / bronchial obstruction: lipoid

HxClinical evaluation

History is the highest-yield step — the cause is often recognizable from it. Most patients present with insidious, progressive exertional dyspnea and a dry cough.

History that narrows the differential

  • Age/sex: ages 20–40 → sarcoidosis, CTD-ILD, LAM (women), PLCH, inherited forms. Older male smokers → IPF.
  • Exposures: occupational dusts (asbestos, silica, beryllium, coal), organic antigens for HP (birds, mould, hot tubs, farming).
  • Drugs: amiodarone, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, bleomycin, immune-checkpoint inhibitors.
  • Smoking: RB-ILD, AMP (DIP), PLCH — and an IPF risk factor.
  • CTD review of systems: Raynaud, arthralgia, myalgia/weakness, dysphagia, rash, sicca, dry eyes/mouth.
  • Tempo: acute/subacute (DAD, OP, acute HP, drug) vs chronic (UIP, fibrotic NSIP); rate of change vs prior imaging.

Examination

  • Often nonspecific. Bibasilar fine "velcro" inspiratory crackles (esp. fibrotic ILD/IPF).
  • Digital clubbing — common in IPF.
  • Advanced disease → signs of pulmonary hypertension / cor pulmonale (loud P2, RV heave, edema).
  • Extrapulmonary CTD clues: Gottron papules, heliotrope rash, mechanic's hands, sclerodactyly, inflammatory arthritis, muscle weakness; nailfold capillaroscopy if Raynaud.

DxDiagnostic testing

No single pathognomonic test. Integrate imaging, physiology, serology, and (selectively) BAL/biopsy in multidisciplinary discussion (MDD) — the diagnostic gold standard.

ModalityRole & key points
HRCTCornerstone. Pattern + distribution (see Imaging key) drives the differential; honeycombing signals UIP and worse prognosis. Compare priors to gauge activity. CXR is insensitive (can be normal in ~10%, esp. HP).
PFTsTypically restrictive: ↓TLC, ↓FVC with reduced DLCO (often earliest/most sensitive). Add resting + exertional oximetry / 6-min walk. DLCO reduced out of proportion → coexisting PH or emphysema.
SerologyRoutine ANA, RF, anti-CCP. Extend when CTD suspected: myositis/antisynthetase (Jo-1), anti-MDA-5, SSA/SSB, Scl-70, CK/aldolase. ILD can precede myositis in ~70% of antisynthetase syndrome.
BALSelective — useful for suspected HP, sarcoidosis, infection, or alveolar hemorrhage. Hemoptysis + radiographic ILD → prompt BAL (sequential lavage) to confirm alveolar bleeding / exclude infection.
Lung biopsyWhen MDD cannot reach a confident diagnosis and the result would change management. Individualize (surgical vs transbronchial cryobiopsy) against procedural risk. Favored for atypical/progressive features (fever, weight loss, hemoptysis, vasculitis signs).
CardiacEcho to screen for PH and assess RV/LV function in dyspnea out of proportion to parenchymal disease.

▦Pattern atlas — clinical · radiological · pathological + imaging

Table 2 features paired with the statement's figures (H&E histology + HRCT). Fibrotic vs non-fibrotic subtypes noted where the update distinguishes them.

UIP — usual interstitial pneumonia

InterstitialFibrotic
Clinical

Chronic onset, high likelihood of progression; potential for rapid worsening & acute exacerbation. More frequent in older males with smoking history, particularly IPF.

Radiology

Subpleural & usually lower predominance. Reticulation, peripheral traction bronchiectasis, and honeycombing.

Pathology

Dense fibrosis with fibroblast foci, subpleural/paraseptal predilection; architectural distortion & honeycombing juxtaposed with normal lung (spatial/temporal heterogeneity).

NSIP — nonspecific interstitial pneumonia

InterstitialCellularFibrotic
Clinical

Chronic onset, variable risk of progression; potential for rapid worsening especially with concurrent OP. More common in younger females, often with CTD.

Radiology

Lower-lobe predominance, commonly subpleural sparing. Ground-glass, fine reticulation, traction bronchiectasis — no honeycombing.

Pathology

Cellular: lymphocytic interstitial infiltrate, alveolar-wall thickening, no fibrosis/granulomas. Fibrotic: diffuse uniform wall fibrosis, traction bronchiolectasis; no bronchiolocentricity/honeycombing/granulomas.

BIP — bronchiolocentric interstitial pneumonia (new 2025)

InterstitialNon-fibroticFibrotic
Clinical

Non-fibrotic: acute/subacute, respiratory + systemic symptoms. Fibrotic: chronic, variable progression; rapid worsening with ongoing exposure. No clear age/sex predilection; look for HP-antigen exposure.

Radiology

Non-fibrotic: centrilobular ground-glass nodules, mosaic attenuation, lobular air trapping. Fibrotic: peribronchovascular ground-glass, traction bronchiectasis, three-density sign.

Pathology

Non-fibrotic: bronchiolocentric chronic inflammation ± non-necrotizing granulomas. Fibrotic: bronchiolocentric fibrosis, peribronchiolar metaplasia.

Overarching airway-centerd pattern seen in HP, CTD-ILD, aspiration, drug-induced ILD. "HP" is now reserved for the multidisciplinary diagnosis, not the pattern.

DAD — diffuse alveolar damage (idiopathic DAD = former AIP)

InterstitialAcute → fibrotic
Clinical

Acute worsening, high likelihood of residual fibrosis and ongoing progression. Risk factors vary by cause — sepsis, aspiration, pneumonia, CTD-ILD.

Radiology

Patchy/diffuse ground-glass that may progress to fibrosis with significant traction bronchiectasis, distortion and honeycombing.

Pathology

Exudative: septal edema, pneumocyte hyperplasia, hyaline membranes. Organizing: loose organizing connective tissue, pneumocyte proliferation. Fibrotic: interstitial fibrosis.

PPFE — pleuroparenchymal fibroelastosis

InterstitialFibrotic
Clinical

Chronic onset, variable progression; frequently associated with significant weight loss. No clear age/sex predilection.

Radiology

Biapical pleural thickening, dense subpleural fibrosis, traction bronchiectasis, upper-zone volume loss with hilar retraction; platythorax, deep sternal notch. Often coexists with UIP.

Pathology

Upper-zone pleural fibrosis with subpleural intra-alveolar fibroelastosis. ≥⅓ of cases coexist with UIP/NSIP/BIP, granulomas, or myco/fungal infection.

LIP — lymphoid interstitial pneumonia

Interstitial
Clinical

Insidious onset, slow progression. No clear age/sex predilection for idiopathic LIP; secondary risk factors vary (CTD, immune deficiency).

Radiology

Mid-to-lower thin-walled cysts with reticulation, ground-glass, bronchovascular & perilymphatic thickening; cysts may be the dominant finding.

Pathology

Dense diffuse lymphoplasmacytic interstitial infiltrate expanding septa, peribronchiolar lymphoid follicles ± germinal centers. Polyclonal; Dutcher bodies absent.

Organizing pneumonia — idiopathic = COP

Alveolar filling
Clinical

Acute/subacute onset, respiratory ± constitutional symptoms. Typically reversible; residual fibrosis possible. Higher recurrence/progression for some causes (esp. CTD).

Radiology

Patchy consolidation with air bronchograms, ground-glass, nodules/mass-like lesions; halo & reversed halo (atoll) sign, perilobular pattern.

Pathology

Intra-alveolar buds of loose connective tissue in alveoli/ducts ± respiratory bronchioles. Mild interstitial inflammation in COP.

RB-ILD — respiratory bronchiolitis–ILD

Alveolar filling
Clinical

Subacute onset; may improve/resolve with smoking cessation. Adult smokers, slight male predominance.

Radiology

Upper-lobe centrilobular ground-glass nodularity.

Pathology

Lightly pigmented macrophages in airspaces with mild inflammation; bronchiolocentric distribution. Lymphoid aggregates/fibrosis may be present.

AMP — alveolar macrophage pneumonia (replaces DIP)

Alveolar filling
Clinical

Highly variable onset, progression in a subset. Adult smokers, slight male predominance (rare in non-smokers).

Radiology

Mid-to-lower predominant, sometimes peripheral. Patchy/confluent ground-glass with smooth reticulation; cysts within ground-glass, emphysema, traction bronchiectasis.

Pathology

Lightly pigmented macrophages filling airspaces with mild inflammation; diffuse distribution (vs bronchiolocentric in RB-ILD).

Combined & unclassifiable patterns

InterstitialAlveolar filling

Multiple patterns frequently coexist — e.g. NSIP + OP (classic for antisynthetase syndrome), UIP + PPFE. Report each contributing pattern. Unclassifiable ILD applies when no single pattern/diagnosis reaches >50% confidence; reassess over time as behavior and data evolve.

RxManagement

Diagnosis-specific and MDD-guided. Broad principles below; always confirm current drug approvals/dosing against a live reference before prescribing.

Treatment principles by driver

  • Remove the cause: exposure- or drug-related ILD (drug pneumonitis, acute HP) → stop the antigen/drug ± a limited glucocorticoid course. Smoking-related (RB-ILD, AMP, PLCH) → smoking cessation is primary.
  • Anti-inflammatory / immunosuppression: glucocorticoids ± steroid-sparing agents (mycophenolate, azathioprine, rituximab) for inflammatory phenotypes — CTD-ILD, NSIP, OP. Not for IPF.
  • Antifibrotics: pirfenidone and nintedanib for IPF; nintedanib also for SSc-ILD and PF-ILD (INBUILD). Nerandomilast (Jascayd, PDE4B inhibitor) — FDA-approved 2025 for both IPF and PPF, the first new IPF drug in over a decade; can be used with an existing antifibrotic.

Progressive pulmonary fibrosis (PPF) — 2022 ATS/ERS/JRS/ALAT

In a patient with non-IPF fibrotic ILD, PPF = ≥2 of 3 within the past year (no alternative explanation): (1) physiologic progression — absolute FVC decline ≥5% predicted or DLCO decline ≥10% predicted; (2) worsening respiratory symptoms; (3) radiologic progression of fibrosis. PPF supports antifibrotic therapy (nintedanib) to slow FVC decline.

General care

  • Vaccinate: pneumococcal, annual influenza, COVID-19, RSV (≥60 yr), Tdap.
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation and supplemental O₂ for exertional/resting hypoxaemia.
  • Treat comorbidities: GERD, OSA, PH, CAD/HF, and heightened lung-cancer risk.
  • Awareness of increased VTE risk.

Monitoring & escalation

  • Serial FVC + DLCO, symptoms, and HRCT; short-interval follow-up if worsening.
  • Drug safety: nintedanib — LFTs pre, monthly ×3, then q3mo; diarrhoea. Pirfenidone — LFTs monthly ×6 then q3mo; photosensitivity, GI upset. Glucocorticoids — glucose, bone, infection.
  • Acute exacerbation / hospitalization for respiratory failure carries >50% 1-yr mortality; use lung-protective ventilation if ventilated.

Advanced disease: refer early for lung-transplant evaluation in progressive fibrotic ILD (poor prognosis, acute-exacerbation risk); list on progression. Palliative/end-of-life: manage dyspnea and cough; consider hospice when life expectancy ≤6 months. Markers of end-stage disease: worsening PH/cor pulmonale, weight loss >10%, resting tachycardia.

Rx+Treatment by disease & antifibrotic details

Two levers: anti-inflammatory/immunosuppression (inflammatory phenotypes) and antifibrotic (IPF and progressive fibrosis). Many fibrotic CTD-ILDs now get both.

Nintedanib (tyrosine-kinase inhibitor)

  • 150 mg BID (100 mg BID if reduced tolerance/hepatic). LFTs baseline, monthly ×3, then q3mo.
  • Diarrhea (very common — manage early), nausea, weight loss; bleeding/VTE caution; teratogenic.

Pirfenidone (antifibrotic)

  • Titrate to 801 mg TID over ~2 weeks. LFTs monthly ×6, then q3mo.
  • Photosensitivity (sun protection), GI upset, anorexia/weight loss.

Nerandomilast (PDE4B inhibitor) new 2025

  • Oral BID; approved for IPF (Oct 2025) and PPF (Dec 2025). Slowed FVC decline in FIBRONEER-IPF/-ILD.
  • No routine LFT monitoring required; diarrhea common. May be added to nintedanib/pirfenidone; dose-adjust with pirfenidone; avoid in Child-Pugh C. Confirm current label.

AEAcute exacerbation of ILD

The feared complication of fibrotic ILD (esp. IPF) — high mortality, often the terminal event.

Definition (2016 international working group)

  • Acute (<1 month) clinically significant respiratory worsening.
  • New bilateral GGO/consolidation on HRCT superimposed on a background fibrotic pattern.
  • Not fully explained by heart failure/fluid overload.
  • Triggered (infection, aspiration, drug, post-procedure) or idiopathic.

Workup & management

  • Exclude infection (incl. BAL if safe), PE (CT-PE), and heart failure.
  • Supportive O₂/HFNC; high-dose glucocorticoids commonly used (low-quality evidence).
  • Avoid injurious ventilation; frank prognosis and early palliative discussion.
  • In-hospital mortality ~50%; a driver of transplant urgency.

Collard HR, et al. Acute exacerbation of IPF — international working group report, 2016. Beyond the classification statement.

DNAMolecular & genetics

The statement highlights emerging molecular tools; a few are already clinically actionable.

Short-telomere syndromes

  • Telomere-biology genes: TERT, TERC, RTEL1, PARN, TINF2. Clues: familial pulmonary fibrosis, premature graying, cytopenias, cryptogenic cirrhosis.
  • Immunosuppression may worsen outcomes in short-telomere patients — flag before MMF and for transplant planning.

Risk alleles & classifiers

  • MUC5B promoter SNP (rs35705950): one T allele ↑ fibrosis risk ~6×, two copies ~20×; shared across IPF-UIP, HP-UIP, RA-UIP — yet paradoxically better survival.
  • HLA-DRB1*15 links some interstitial pneumonias to immune mechanisms; abnormal genes in up to ~10% of "sporadic" cases.
  • Genomic classifier (gene-expression + machine learning) can recognize a UIP signature on transbronchial biopsy — strengthens a UIP diagnosis when honeycombing is absent; broad clinical utility still unproven.

★Attending-level tips & tricks

Practical judgment that lives outside the classification document — MDD craft, pattern traps, and management landmines.

Reading UIP with the IPF-guideline lens

  • Radiologic tiers: typical UIP · probable UIP · indeterminate · alternative diagnosis. In the right clinical context, probable UIP is managed like typical — biopsy often unnecessary.
  • Traction bronchiectasis = fibrosis; it separates fibrotic NSIP/UIP from purely inflammatory disease and tracks progression.
  • Basal + subpleural + honeycombing with a compatible history (older, ex-smoker) → confident IPF without tissue.

Hunt for HP even without an exposure

  • CT tells: mosaic attenuation, three-density sign, centrilobular ground-glass nodules, air trapping, upper/mid zones.
  • Exposure history: birds/feathers/down, mold/damp, hot tubs, humidifiers, occupational antigens — re-ask on every visit.
  • BAL lymphocytosis (>30–40%) supports HP, cellular NSIP, or sarcoid. Serum IgG antigen panels have limited specificity.

Always exclude CTD (it changes everything)

  • Exam + ANA, RF, anti-CCP baseline; extend to myositis/antisynthetase panel when suspicious.
  • Anti-MDA5 → rapidly progressive ILD / (amyopathic) dermatomyositis — poor prognosis, act fast. Antisynthetase → NSIP+OP overlap.
  • Consider the IPAF construct when features fall short of a defined CTD (dedicated criteria; separate document).

Management landmines

  • Do not immunosuppress IPF — PANTHER-IPF showed prednisone/azathioprine/NAC increased death & hospitalization.
  • Short-telomere phenotype (familial fibrosis, premature graying, cytopenias, cryptogenic cirrhosis) tolerates immunosuppression poorly — worse outcomes; flag before mycophenolate and for transplant planning.
  • Antifibrotics slow, not reverse, decline: nintedanib (diarrhea, LFTs) · pirfenidone (photosensitivity, GI).

Don't miss the vascular / acute story

  • DLCO out of proportion to volumes → think CPFE or pulmonary hypertension; get an echo, consider PH referral.
  • Acute exacerbation (new ground-glass on background fibrosis): exclude infection/PE/heart failure; high mortality; supportive ± steroids; lung-protective ventilation if intubated.

Prognosis, biopsy & the PPF lever

  • GAP index (gender, age, FVC, DLCO) for baseline mortality risk; serial FVC/DLCO trend matters more than any single value.
  • Transbronchial cryobiopsy increasingly substitutes for surgical lung biopsy at experienced centers (lower morbidity, good MDD yield).
  • PPF in non-IPF fibrotic ILD (≥2 of: FVC↓≥5%, symptoms, radiologic progression / yr) unlocks antifibrotic therapy — screen for it at each visit.

These tips draw on IPF/PPF guidelines (ATS/ERS/JRS/ALAT 2018, 2022), PANTHER-IPF, GAP, and telomere-biology literature beyond the classification statement — verify specifics against current guidance before acting.
