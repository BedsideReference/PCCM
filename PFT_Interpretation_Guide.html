NEWWhat changed from the 2005 standard

Interpretation has three tasks: (1) classify a value as normal vs abnormal relative to a healthy population, (2) translate abnormalities into a physiological pattern, (3) integrate with clinical data. This standard covers (1) and (2). The headline shift: away from % predicted and fixed cut-offs, toward z-scores and the LLN derived from GLI reference equations.

Reference equations. GLI equations for spirometry, lung volumes and DLCO (replacing NHANES III / no-equation defaults).
Normal range. Define abnormal by LLN = 5th percentile (z = −1.645). FEV₁/FVC <0.70 and <80% predicted no longer recommended.
Bronchodilator response. >10% change relative to the predicted value (FEV₁ or FVC) — replaces ≥12% and 200 mL from baseline.
Severity grading. Grade by z-score across all indices (legacy FEV₁ %-predicted bands retained as alternative).
Race/ethnicity. Moves away from race-specific equations. Update — ATS/ERS 2023: a subsequent official statement recommends race-neutral GLI Global spirometry equations; switching reclassifies a meaningful number of Black and SE-Asian patients (more obstruction/air trapping detected). Avoid embedding race as biology; interpret in clinical context.
Biological sex. Use biological sex, not gender, to select reference equations.

RefReference values & the normal range

A measured value is interpreted against the distribution of healthy people of the same age, height and biological sex. The z-score expresses how many SDs a value sits from the predicted mean; the LLN (5th percentile) corresponds to z = −1.645.

Why z-score, not % predicted

  • % predicted ignores that variability differs by age, height and index — a flat "80%" over- or under-calls abnormality depending on the person.
  • z-score accounts for the age-/height-specific scatter, so a single threshold (−1.645 = LLN) applies uniformly.
  • LLN = 5th percentile ⇒ ~5% of healthy people fall below by definition (interpret near-threshold values cautiously — the "zone of uncertainty").

GLI equations & the race-neutral shift

  • The 2022 standard used population-specific GLI groups (White, Black, SE-Asian, NE-Asian) plus a "Multi-ethnic" average.
  • Current best practice (ATS/ERS 2023 statement): use race-neutral GLI Global spirometry equations — race/ethnicity is a social construct poorly correlated with lung physiology, and race-specific equations can under-diagnose disease in Black and other groups.
  • Switching to GLI Global detects more obstruction/air trapping in previously race-adjusted groups; always document which equation the lab uses. (Validated race-neutral equations for lung volumes/DLCO are less established.)

Preferred approach: grade by z-score, applied to any index (obstruction or restriction). Legacy FEV₁ %-predicted bands and a DLCO scheme are retained as alternatives.

Pearl. Severity is graded on the index that defines the impairment (e.g. FEV₁ for obstruction, TLC or FVC for restriction, DLCO for gas transfer) — not on the FEV₁/FVC ratio itself.

SpiSpirometry — patterns

Start with FEV₁/FVC (use the largest VC), then FVC. Ratio and FVC together sort patients into normal, obstruction, possible restriction/non-specific, or possible mixed — the last two need lung volumes to resolve.

High yield PRISm — preserved ratio impaired spirometry

Definition

FEV₁ < LLN with a preserved FEV₁/FVC (≥ LLN). A spirometry-only label — not a diagnosis. Prevalence ~7–12% of adults; more common with obesity and current/former smoking.

Why it matters

  • Not benign: associated with more respiratory symptoms, higher all-cause and cardiovascular mortality, and comorbidity.
  • Unstable phenotype: on follow-up a substantial subset transitions to overt obstruction (COPD), others to a restrictive pattern, and some normalise — so repeat spirometry.
  • Always characterise further: full lung volumes + DLCO sort the drivers — true restriction (low TLC: obesity, ILD, neuromuscular/chest-wall) vs early airway disease/air trapping vs submaximal effort.

Overlaps with the "non-specific pattern," but PRISm is defined on FEV₁ + ratio at spirometry, before volumes are known.

F–VFlow–volume loop morphology

Loop shape rapidly flags the pattern and effort quality before numbers are read.

  • Obstruction: coved/concave (scooped) expiratory limb; reduced peak flow; the more concave, the more small-airway involvement.
  • Restriction: tall, narrow loop with preserved/high peak flow but reduced volume (convex expiratory limb reflecting high elastic recoil).
  • Variable intrathoracic upper-airway obstruction: expiratory plateau. Variable extrathoracic: inspiratory plateau. Fixed: plateau on both limbs.
  • Poor effort / submaximal: non-reproducible, rounded, or truncated loops.

BDRBronchodilator responsiveness

New 2022 definition removes the baseline-dependent bias of the old rule.

Definition

A positive response = change >10% relative to the predicted value for FEV₁ or FVC.

%change = (post − pre) / predicted × 100

Replaces the old "≥12% and ≥200 mL from baseline," which over-called responses in people with low baselines and under-called them in large lungs.

Interpretation caveats

  • BDR is a continuum — avoid over-reliance on the strict cut-off (same zone-of-uncertainty problem as any threshold).
  • A negative test does not exclude asthma, nor does a positive test confirm it or exclude COPD.
  • Absence of acute BDR doesn't predict lack of clinical benefit from bronchodilators.

VolLung volumes

TLC is the arbiter of restriction. Plethysmography preferred; compare alveolar volume (VA) from the DLCO manoeuvre with plethysmographic TLC — a gap suggests maldistribution/air trapping.

  • Restriction: TLC < LLN (5th percentile) with a normal FEV₁/VC. Simple restriction = low TLC with proportionate FVC; complex restriction = low TLC with disproportionately preserved/low FVC (suggests coexisting airway or neuromuscular process).
  • Hyperinflation: elevated TLC. Air trapping: elevated RV and RV/TLC — hallmark of obstruction.
  • Mixed: FEV₁/VC and TLC both < LLN.
  • Non-specific pattern: low FVC with normal TLC and ratio — often obesity, neuromuscular/chest-wall disease, or submaximal effort.

DLCOGas transfer (DLCO / TLCO)

DLCO reflects the alveolar–capillary membrane, capillary blood volume and haemoglobin. Always correct for Hb (and COHb/back-pressure) before interpreting. Partition a low DLCO with KCO (DLCO/VA) and VA.

ΔtChange over time

Third interpretive task, applied longitudinally. The standard stresses that a single "meaningful change" threshold is not yet established.

  • Ideally compare to the patient's own pre-disease baseline; failing that, to the decline expected in healthy peers.
  • Accelerated decline predicts poor outcomes regardless of baseline — trend matters as much as absolute value.
  • Change tools differ by group: conditional change score in children; FEV₁Q (FEV₁ over a lower reference value) in adults.
  • Natural within-person variability (day-to-day, week-to-week, year-to-year) is substantial and under-defined — avoid over-interpreting small changes; a relative FVC decline of ~10% is a commonly used disease-progression signal (e.g. fibrotic ILD).

AdvPulmonologist pearls & advanced testing

Beyond the core interpretive algorithm — quality gating, provocation, muscle testing, and pattern traps that change management.

Quality first (ATS/ERS 2019) — don't interpret unacceptable data

  • Need ≥3 acceptable manoeuvres; the two largest FVC values and the two largest FEV₁ values must each agree within 150 mL (100 mL if ≤6 yr) for a top (grade A) result.
  • Acceptable effort: good start with back-extrapolated volume <5% of FVC or <100 mL (whichever greater), no cough in the first second, and an end-of-test plateau (≥1 s of no volume change) or forced expiration ≥15 s.
  • Report the largest FVC and largest FEV₁ even if from different efforts. A "U" (usable) grade may still inform care.
Pearl. A "restrictive-looking" low FVC with a poor plateau or short expiratory time is often submaximal effort/early termination, not true restriction — check the volume–time curve before calling restriction.

Bronchoprovocation — normal spirometry but asthma suspected

  • Direct (methacholine): report PD₂₀ (delivered dose; ERS 2017 standard favours PD₂₀ over PC₂₀). High sensitivity — a negative test largely excludes current asthma; a positive test is not specific.
  • Indirect (mannitol, exercise, eucapnic voluntary hyperpnoea, hypertonic saline): more specific for asthma / exercise-induced bronchoconstriction and track airway inflammation.

Respiratory muscle strength — unexplained restriction, normal parenchyma

  • MIP/MEP (maximal inspiratory/expiratory pressures) and SNIP (sniff nasal inspiratory pressure).
  • Supine FVC fall ≥20% vs upright suggests diaphragmatic weakness.
  • Signature: restriction (low TLC) with preserved/high KCO and low MIP/MEP — points extrapulmonary, not parenchymal.

Pattern traps

  • CPFE (combined pulmonary fibrosis + emphysema): emphysematous hyperinflation offsets fibrotic restriction → "pseudonormal" spirometry and lung volumes with a markedly reduced DLCO out of proportion; high pulmonary-hypertension risk. Don't be reassured by normal volumes.
  • Volume responders: bronchodilator response can appear as an FVC increase (reduced air trapping) rather than FEV₁ — common in COPD; check both.
  • KCO nuance: KCO rises non-linearly as VA falls, so a "normal" KCO cannot correct a low DLCO at low VA; normal/high KCO with low VA suggests extrapulmonary restriction, pneumonectomy, or incomplete breath.
  • Isolated low DLCO (normal spirometry & volumes) → pulmonary vascular disease (PAH/CTEPH/PVOD), anaemia (correct for Hb), or early ILD/emphysema.
  • Flow–volume shape: sawtooth oscillations → upper-airway instability (OSA, tracheomalacia); fixed plateau on both limbs → fixed large-airway obstruction.
